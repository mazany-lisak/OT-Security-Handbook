# Purpose

This document provides a security architecture and **compensating-control** reference for the Siemens **SIMATIC S7-400** high-end programmable automation controller, from the perspective of an OT Security Architect.

The S7-400 is the **large-scale, high-performance sibling** of the [S7-300.md](../S7-300/S7-300.md): the platform Siemens positioned for continuous process control, large discrete lines and — in its **H-systems (fault-tolerant, redundant)** and **F-systems (fail-safe)** variants — the backbone of many refining, chemical, power, water and pharmaceutical control rooms, frequently paired with **SIMATIC PCS 7** as a DCS-like environment. It is now **discontinued by Siemens** (end of manufacture; runout of spares/support in progress across the range), yet — precisely because it was chosen for the largest, most consequential processes — remains embedded in some of the highest-criticality installations still documented in this handbook.

The S7-400 **shares the S7-300's fundamental security reality**: unauthenticated S7comm, a mechanical key switch as the primary physical access control, and weak/legacy block protection. Rather than repeat that material, this document is **delegating and differential**: section 1–2 summarise the shared ground and link to [S7-300.md](../S7-300/S7-300.md) for full depth, while the rest of this document concentrates on what is genuinely different at S7-400 scale — **H/R redundancy, larger process topology, and PCS 7 context.**

> **Historical grounding.** The S7-400 family belongs to the same generation and protocol reality as the S7-300 that Stuxnet (2010) targeted; the same unauthenticated-S7comm-plus-compromised-engineering-workstation risk pattern applies with equal force here, at correspondingly larger process consequence. See [S7-300.md](../S7-300/S7-300.md) § 2.2 and [ICS-Kill-Chain.md](../../../07-Threat-Intelligence/ICS-Kill-Chain.md).

> **Verification / lifecycle note.** Siemens has published defined end-of-life milestones for the S7-400 range. Verify the exact CPU/module (including H/F-CPU variant), firmware and current support status of deployed units against Siemens' product lifecycle tools; treat this document as the *pattern*.

# 1 — Overview

## 1.1 Platform Role and Relationship to S7-300

Where the S7-300 is the discrete/machine workhorse, the S7-400 was Siemens' answer for **large, continuous, high-availability processes**: bigger backplanes, higher I/O counts, more communication processors per rack, and — critically — **hot-standby redundant CPU pairs (H-systems)** for processes where an unplanned controller failure is unacceptable. It runs the same protocol family (**S7comm**) with the same absence of native authentication, over the same MPI/PROFIBUS/Ethernet networking options, programmed from the same **STEP 7 V5.x / TIA Portal** toolchain.

**Read [S7-300.md](../S7-300/S7-300.md) first** for the full threat model, compensating-control architecture, identity/protection reality, communications-security detail and migration pattern — all of it applies here unchanged. This document adds only what the S7-400's scale and redundancy genuinely change.

## 1.2 Families

* **Standard CPUs** — non-redundant, large-scale process/discrete control.
* **H-CPUs (S7-400H)** — hot-standby redundant pairs connected by a dedicated **synchronisation link (fibre-optic sync modules)**, providing bumpless failover for continuous processes.
* **F-CPUs / FH-CPUs** — fail-safe (and fail-safe + fault-tolerant) variants for SIL-rated safety functions, often as a **separate SIS** alongside a standard/H basic-process-control CPU.
* **PCS 7 context** — the S7-400 (standard and H) is a common controller choice within **SIMATIC PCS 7**, Siemens' DCS, adding an operator/engineering layer (OS servers, ES engineering stations, Plant/Route control) above the controller layer.

## 1.3 ISA-95 / Purdue Placement

Identical pattern to the S7-300 ([Purdue-Model.md](../../../02-Standards/Purdue-Model.md)): controller(s) at **Level 1**, I/O and field devices at **Level 0**, engineering from a dedicated zone. Where PCS 7 is present, its OS/ES servers sit at **Level 2**, and the same Engineering ↔ Operations boundary discipline applies with added weight — PCS 7 engineering stations can affect an entire process unit, not a single machine.

# 2 — Shared Security Reality (Summary)

The S7-400 inherits, unchanged, the security-capability gap documented in full in [S7-300.md](../S7-300/S7-300.md) §§ 1.4, 2, 4:

* **No native authentication** on S7comm — any reachable host can read, write, and issue start/stop/mode-change functions.
* **No transport encryption**, no operator-exposed firmware integrity, no on-device audit logging.
* Physical/mechanical **key switch** as the closest device-level access control; **legacy block protection** as weak deterrence only.
* **Engineering-workstation compromise** as the highest-consequence attack path (the Stuxnet pattern, equally applicable here).
* The same IEC 62443 framing: an unavoidable **compensating-control case**, with the gap between Target and achieved Security Level documented as residual risk ([Risk-Based-Security.md](../../../03-Architecture/Risk-Based-Security.md)).

Do not re-derive this material per site — apply [S7-300.md](../S7-300/S7-300.md) §§ 2–7 directly to S7-400 zones, then layer the differences below.

# 3 — What Is Different at S7-400 Scale

## 3.1 Consequence Concentration

An S7-400 (especially H-CPU) failure or compromise frequently governs an **entire process unit** — a distillation train, a boiler, a large batch line — rather than one machine. The **consequence side of the risk equation is materially higher** even though the technical vulnerability is identical to the S7-300. Weight zone Target SL and compensating-control investment accordingly ([Risk-Management-Principles.md](../../../00-Core/Risk-Management-Principles.md)).

## 3.2 Larger, More Complex Topology

S7-400 installations typically involve more communication processors, more PROFIBUS/PROFINET segments, and — in PCS 7 — a defined plant-wide network architecture (Terminal Bus, Plant Bus, PCS 7 redundant bus systems). Each additional CP or bus segment is another S7comm-reachable path; the conduit inventory (§5) is correspondingly larger and deserves the same rigor as a full plant network design, not a single-device firewall rule.

## 3.3 More Stakeholders, Slower Change

Continuous processes mean maintenance windows are scarcer and more consequential; engineering changes typically involve process engineering, safety and operations sign-off in addition to the automation team. Change Management for S7-400 zones should explicitly integrate **process-safety Management of Change** ([Change-Management.md](../../../09-Operations/Change-Management.md) → MoC) as standard practice, not an exception.

# 4 — Redundancy-Specific Security

## 4.1 Synchronisation Link

H-systems synchronise process image, memory and event state between the primary and standby CPU over a **dedicated fibre-optic sync link**. Treat this link as a control-critical conduit:

* Physically protect and document the sync-link path; it should not be repurposed, tapped, or run alongside general cabling without explicit design review.
* The sync link is not a substitute for the L1 zone boundary — both CPUs remain individually reachable over S7comm and both require the same containment as a standalone S7-400.
* Include synchronisation loss/re-sync events in monitoring (§6) — an unexpected desync is both an availability and a potential tampering signal.

## 4.2 Switchover as an Attack Consideration

Forced or triggered switchover (primary → standby) is a legitimate maintenance action but also a potential **denial-of-availability or diversion tactic**: an attacker able to force repeated switchovers, or to target the standby CPU while attention is on the primary, can degrade redundancy without ever touching "the" controller in an obvious way. Monitor switchover events and correlate them with any concurrent S7comm write/download activity.

## 4.3 Dual-CPU Configuration Governance

Both CPUs in an H-pair must carry **identical, version-controlled logic and configuration**; configuration drift between primary and standby (from an out-of-process change to only one CPU) undermines both safety and security assumptions. Fold H-pair consistency checks into [Configuration-Management.md](../../../09-Operations/Configuration-Management.md) baselines explicitly — this is a common, under-checked gap in H-system operations.

## 4.4 F/FH Safety Separation

Where a separate F- or FH-CPU implements the Safety Instrumented System alongside the basic-process-control S7-400, maintain it as an **architecturally distinct, most-restricted zone** ([Purdue-Model.md](../../../02-Standards/Purdue-Model.md) → Safety ↔ Process Control boundary); a Basic Process Control System compromise must not be able to reach or influence the safety layer.

# 5 — Communications Security

Full protocol depth in [OT-Protocols.md](../../../04-Network/OT-Protocols.md) and [S7-300.md](../S7-300/S7-300.md) § 5; S7-400/PCS 7-specific additions:

* **S7comm** across every CP and integrated interface — apply the same S7comm-aware DPI/containment per conduit, not just at one "main" interface; large installations often have more S7comm-reachable paths than the network diagram initially shows.
* **PCS 7 Terminal Bus / Plant Bus** — where present, treat as distinct, documented zones with their own conduits into the controller layer; do not let the PCS 7 network architecture default to flat because "it's all one DCS."
* **Redundant bus systems** (where PCS 7 uses redundant Plant Bus switches) — apply the same containment to both paths; redundancy in the network must not become redundancy in the attack surface (i.e. two unmonitored paths instead of one).
* **Sync link** — proprietary, fibre-based; not a routable network protocol, but document it as a conduit in the zone/conduit model regardless (§4.1).

---

# 6 — Monitoring and Logging

Everything in [S7-300.md](../S7-300/S7-300.md) § 6 applies (S7comm writes, program downloads, mode changes, new source hosts, key-switch position, boundary denies). Add for S7-400/H-systems ([SIEM.md](../../../09-Operations/SIEM.md), [Monitoring.md](../../../09-Operations/Monitoring.md)):

* **Switchover and re-synchronisation events**, with source/cause where determinable; unexplained or repeated switchovers are high-priority.
* **Configuration divergence between primary and standby CPUs** — treat as a security-relevant finding, not only a reliability one.
* Where PCS 7 is present, **OS/ES server authentication, engineering sessions and process-value override/force events** at Level 2, correlated with Level 1 controller activity.
* Safety-CPU (F/FH) configuration or force-value changes as the highest-severity detection category in the estate.

---

# 7 — Backup, Spares and Recovery

Apply [S7-300.md](../S7-300/S7-300.md) § 7 in full (offline verified project backups, tool/firmware custody, spares strategy, recovery testing), with two S7-400-specific additions:

* **Both CPUs' configurations** must be backed up and shown identical at time of backup; recovery testing for H-systems should include restoring and re-establishing redundancy, not just restoring a single CPU.
* **Spares scarcity is more acute** for S7-400/H hardware than for S7-300 given lower unit volumes and higher module cost; given the consequence concentration of §3.1, treat spares planning here as a priority item, not a background task.

# 8 — Migration and Decommissioning

* **Target:** [S7-1500.md](../S7-1500/S7-1500.md) (including its **H-CPU / R-CPU redundant variants**) with TIA Portal for standalone or moderate-scale replacement; large PCS 7 estates typically undergo a **DCS-level migration project** (evaluating Siemens PCS neo or equivalent) rather than a like-for-like controller swap — scope this as a distinct, larger modernisation programme ([OT-Lifecycle.md](../../../00-Core/OT-Lifecycle.md) → Modernization), not a routine hardware refresh.
* **Prioritise** by the consequence concentration noted in §3.1: process-critical H-systems with any remote or IT-adjacent reachability are the highest migration priority in a legacy-Siemens estate, ahead of comparable-risk S7-300 units, precisely because of what they control.
* **Decommissioning** follows [S7-300.md](../S7-300/S7-300.md) § 8 and [OT-Lifecycle.md](../../../00-Core/OT-Lifecycle.md); for H-systems, ensure **both** CPUs and the sync-link hardware are addressed — a decommissioned "primary" with a still-wired, still-live standby is a realistic oversight.

---

# 9 — Best Practices and Common Mistakes

**Practices that pay off (in addition to the S7-300 list):** weighting Target SL and compensating-control investment by the larger consequence an S7-400/H typically governs; treating the sync link and both CPUs as one governed unit; monitoring switchover events as a security signal, not just an availability one; integrating process-safety MoC into every S7-400-zone change; scoping large PCS 7 migrations as DCS-level programmes rather than piecemeal controller swaps.

**Common mistakes (in addition to the S7-300 list):**

* Treating H-system redundancy as a security control — it protects availability against hardware failure, not against a network-reachable, unauthenticated write to either CPU.
* Backing up only the "primary" CPU, or never verifying the standby matches it.
* PCS 7 Terminal/Plant Bus architected flat because the whole system is nominally "the DCS."
* No monitoring of switchover/re-sync events, missing a slow-burn denial-of-availability pattern.
* Scoping a PCS 7 controller-layer migration as a simple hardware swap and discovering the DCS-level dependencies mid-project.

# Architect Notes

The S7-400 does not introduce a new technical vulnerability beyond what [S7-300.md](../S7-300/S7-300.md) already documents — it introduces **scale of consequence**. The same unauthenticated S7comm, the same engineering-workstation dependency, the same weak block protection: all present, all unchanged. What changes is what happens if they are exploited — often an entire continuous process unit rather than one machine, frequently with a safety-instrumented layer nearby that must remain provably independent. Resource compensating controls, monitoring and migration priority accordingly: same architecture, higher stakes.

# Decision Support

Apply [S7-300.md](../S7-300/S7-300.md) § *Decision Support* to every S7-400 unit, plus:

1. Is the consequence of each S7-400/H-system's compromise or failure explicitly assessed at process-unit scale, not device scale?
2. Is the synchronisation link documented as a conduit and physically protected?
3. Are both CPUs in an H-pair backed up, version-matched and included in recovery testing together?
4. Are switchover/re-synchronisation events monitored and investigated?
5. Where PCS 7 is present, is the Terminal/Plant Bus architecture zoned and conduited explicitly, not treated as one flat DCS network?
6. Is the F/FH safety layer architecturally isolated from the basic-process-control S7-400?
7. Is a large-PCS7-migration scoped as a DCS-level programme with appropriate governance, distinct from routine hardware refresh?

# AI Guidance

When discussing Siemens S7-400:

* First apply everything in S7-300.md's AI Guidance — the threat model, compensating-control framing, and Stuxnet-era risk pattern are identical.
* Add the scale dimension explicitly: S7-400/H-systems typically govern a whole process unit, so weight recommended compensating controls, monitoring priority and migration urgency higher than for an equivalent S7-300 finding.
* Never present H-system redundancy as a cybersecurity control; it addresses hardware availability only — both CPUs still require the same network containment individually.
* For PCS 7 contexts, recommend zoning the Terminal Bus/Plant Bus explicitly rather than treating the DCS as one flat network, and scope migration as a DCS-level programme.
* Keep the safety (F/FH) layer framed as an architecturally separate, most-restricted zone, consistent with the handbook's Safety ↔ Process Control boundary principle.
* Delegate shared fundamentals to S7-300.md and protocol depth to OT-Protocols.md; verify CPU/H-system/PCS 7 specifics and Siemens ProductCERT advisories against current vendor sources rather than asserting them.
