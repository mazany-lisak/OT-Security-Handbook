# Purpose

This document provides an engineering-oriented overview of the IEC 62443 series from the perspective of an OT Security Architect.

IEC 62443 is the primary international standard for cybersecurity in Industrial Automation and Control Systems (IACS). It is the engineering implementation framework that supports compliance with regulatory obligations defined in NIS2 and the Czech Cybersecurity Act.

This document explains how to apply IEC 62443 throughout the full IACS lifecycle — from initial concept through design, procurement, implementation, commissioning, operation, and decommissioning — with practical guidance drawn from real OT projects across energy, water, transport and manufacturing sectors.

The Czech Cybersecurity Act and its decrees define the legal obligations. IEC 62443 provides the engineering methodology to satisfy them. These are complementary, not competing.

# What Is IEC 62443?

IEC 62443 (formerly ISA-99) is an international series of standards for the security of Industrial Automation and Control Systems (IACS), developed jointly by ISA (International Society of Automation) and IEC (International Electrotechnical Commission).

The standard family is maintained by the ISA99 committee and published under both ISA and IEC designations (IEC 62443 = ISA/IEC 62443).

## Why IEC 62443 Instead of ISO 27001 or NIST CSF?

Unlike enterprise cybersecurity frameworks, IEC 62443 was designed specifically for industrial environments where:

| OT Characteristic | Implication for Security Design |
|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Safety is critical** | Cyber incidents can cause physical harm, environmental damage, or loss of life |
| **Availability is primary** | Production downtime has direct financial and safety consequences — security must not impair availability |
| **Long system lifecycles** | Systems may operate for 20–30 years; security must be maintainable over that horizon |
| **Legacy equipment** | PLCs, RTUs and HMIs without modern security capabilities are the norm, not the exception |
| **Operational constraints on patching** | Systems cannot always be taken offline; patch cycles measured in months or years |
| **Real-time performance** | Security controls must not introduce latency that affects control loop timing |
| **Proprietary protocols** | Modbus, DNP3, PROFINET, EtherNet/IP, IEC 61850 — not HTTP/HTTPS |
| **Physical consequences** | A compromised PLC can damage equipment, injure people, or cause environmental incidents |

ISO 27001 addresses information security management for IT environments. IEC 62443 addresses operational technology where the primary concern is not data confidentiality but physical process safety and availability.

# Stakeholder Roles

IEC 62443 assigns responsibilities to three stakeholder groups. In real OT projects, these roles often overlap and must be explicitly assigned in contracts.

| Role | Primary Responsibility | Key Standards | Typical Examples |
|------|----------------------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Asset Owner** | Secure operation, governance, risk management | IEC 62443-2-1, 2-5, 3-2, 3-3 | Utilities, manufacturers, process industry operators |
| **System Integrator** | Secure architecture design and system implementation | IEC 62443-2-4, 3-2, 3-3 | Engineering companies, SI firms, automation integrators |
| **Product Supplier** | Secure product development and maintenance | IEC 62443-4-1, 4-2 | PLC manufacturers (Siemens, Rockwell, ABB), SCADA vendors |

> **Common project problem:** In many OT projects, responsibilities overlap without being explicitly assigned. The asset owner assumes the integrator handles security; the integrator assumes the product supplier provides secure-by-default components; the product supplier assumes the integrator hardens the deployment. Result: nobody is accountable. IEC 62443 requires explicit contractual assignment of responsibilities.

# Key Documents for OT Architects — In-Depth

## IEC 62443-1-1: Terminology and Concepts

The definitional foundation. Introduces the core vocabulary including Zone, Conduit, IACS, System Under Consideration (SuC), Security Level (SL), Target SL (SL-T), Capability SL (SL-C), and Achieved SL (SL-A).

**Read this first.** Misuse of terminology leads to misaligned architectures and contract disputes.

---

## IEC 62443-2-1: Security Management System for IACS

Defines what an asset owner must establish to manage IACS cybersecurity systematically.

Key requirements:
* Cybersecurity management system (CSMS) — analogous to ISO 27001 ISMS but adapted for OT.
* Risk management process.
* Security policy framework.
* Personnel security (roles, training, awareness).
* Business continuity and disaster recovery.
* Incident response.
* Compliance and audit.

**Architecture implication:** The CSMS must explicitly cover OT assets, not only IT. A separate OT security policy framework is typically required alongside (not as a subset of) the IT ISMS.

---

## IEC 62443-2-3: Patch Management in the IACS Environment

One of the most practically relevant standards for OT operations. Defines a structured patch management process that accounts for OT constraints.

Key elements:
* Patch source assessment (vendor communication channels, authenticity verification).
* Patch impact assessment (compatibility, regression risk for real-time systems).
* Patch testing in a staging environment before production deployment.
* Emergency patching procedures for critical vulnerabilities.
* Compensating controls documentation for systems that cannot be patched.
* Patch deployment in maintenance windows coordinated with production scheduling.

**OT reality:** A patch that crashes a PLC or DCS during production is worse than the vulnerability it fixes. IEC 62443-2-3 provides the framework to avoid this through systematic risk-based patch management.

---

## IEC 62443-2-4: Requirements for IACS Solution Suppliers (System Integrators)

Defines security capability requirements for organizations that design, implement and commission IACS. This standard is the primary tool for evaluating and qualifying system integrators.

Key requirement areas:
* Security management during project execution.
* Secure engineering practices.
* Security hardening of delivered systems.
* Handling of security vulnerabilities discovered during projects.
* Documentation delivered to asset owner.
* Handover security activities.

**Practical use:** IEC 62443-2-4 compliance should be a mandatory qualification criterion in RFP/tender documents for OT system integrators. Ask integrators to provide evidence of their IEC 62443-2-4 capability — documented processes, past project examples, or third-party certification.

---

## IEC 62443-3-2: Security Risk Assessment for System Design

The central methodology for linking risk to architecture. Defines how to perform a Security Level Risk Assessment (SLRA).

This is the standard that directly produces the zone/conduit design, Security Level Targets, and the security architecture. See the dedicated section below for the full SLRA process.

---

## IEC 62443-3-3: System Security Requirements and Security Levels

Defines system-level security requirements structured by Foundational Requirements (FR) and System Requirements (SR), each specified for Security Levels 1–4.

This standard is the primary reference for defining what security capabilities a system must achieve at a given Security Level Target. See the Foundational Requirements section below for detail.

---

## IEC 62443-4-1: Secure Product Development Lifecycle

Defines security requirements for the processes used to develop IACS products. Addresses the product supplier role.

Key practice areas:
* Security management during development.
* Specification of security requirements.
* Secure design (threat modeling, secure design principles).
* Secure implementation (coding standards, static analysis).
* Security verification and validation testing.
* Management of security-related issues (vulnerability handling, PSIRT).
* Security update management.
* Security guidelines for end users.

**Asset owner use:** When procuring IACS products (PLC firmware, SCADA software, DCS), require evidence of IEC 62443-4-1 compliance from suppliers. This establishes that the product was developed with security in mind — not only that it has security features.

---

## IEC 62443-4-2: Technical Security Requirements for IACS Components

Defines component-level security requirements (not system-level). Applies to individual products: controllers (PLCs, RTUs), devices (HMIs, field devices with network interfaces), host devices (engineering workstations, SCADA servers), and network devices (industrial switches, firewalls).

Requirements are structured by the same FR/SR model as IEC 62443-3-3, mapped to component capability.

**Architecture use:** When a component's Capability SL (SL-C) does not meet the zone's Target SL (SL-T), the gap must be closed through compensating controls at the system or zone level.

# Core Concepts in Depth

## Zones and Conduits

The Zone and Conduit model is the foundational architectural framework of IEC 62443. Everything else — Security Levels, Foundational Requirements, risk assessment — ultimately results in a zone/conduit design.

### Zone Definition

A **Zone** is a logical or physical grouping of assets that:
* share the same security requirements,
* have the same trust level,
* are protected by the same security policy.

**Zone design principles:**
* Assets with different Security Level requirements belong in different zones.
* Zones are defined by function and trust — not purely by network topology or VLAN membership.
* Every zone has an explicitly assigned Security Level Target (SL-T).
* Zone boundaries must be enforced by conduits — not just documented.
* The zone with the lowest Security Level in a communication path determines the effective security of that path.

### Zone Design Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Consequence | Correction |
|---------|------------|------------|
| Mixing safety and basic control in one zone | Compromise of BPCS reaches SIS | Physically separate SIS zone with hardware data diode |
| Engineering WS in same zone as SCADA servers | Malware from engineering laptop reaches SCADA | Separate engineering zone with controlled conduit |
| Historian in control zone | Historian connectivity creates IT↔OT path bypassing DMZ | Historian in MOM zone; read-only replica in DMZ |
| VLAN-only separation without ACL | Layer 2 hop can bypass VLAN | Routed separation with ACL or dedicated firewall |
| Assuming zone = VLAN | VLANs are network config, not security zones | Zones require enforced conduits, not just VLAN tags |

### Conduit Definition

A **Conduit** is a managed communication path between two zones. It is the only permissible mechanism for inter-zone communication.

A conduit is not:
* A network cable.
* A switch port.
* An open firewall rule.

A conduit is:
* A defined, documented, and enforced security boundary.
* Associated with a specific set of allowed communications (source, destination, protocol, port, direction).
* Monitored for anomalous traffic.
* Subject to periodic review and reauthorization.

### Conduit Implementation Options

| Conduit Type | Appropriate Use | Notes |
|-------------|----------------|-------|
| Industrial firewall (allowlist) | General inter-zone communication with bidirectional need | Deny-by-default; every rule documented and reviewed |
| Unidirectional gateway / data diode | OT→IT data transfer where no return path is needed | Physically enforced; cannot be misconfigured to allow return traffic |
| OPC UA gateway | Structured data exchange between control and MOM zones | Apply OPC UA security profile (Sign and Encrypt); filter by node ID |
| Encrypted VPN tunnel | Remote access, WAN-connected sites | MFA required; terminate in DMZ, not in control zone |
| Application-layer proxy | Protocol translation (OPC DA → OPC UA, Modbus → REST) | Protocol-aware inspection; converts insecure protocols at boundary |
| DMZ buffer architecture | When bidirectional data exchange is unavoidable | Two-firewall DMZ; no direct path through |

## Security Levels (SL) — Complete Reference

### Security Level Definitions

| SL | Threat Actor Profile | Resources | Skills | Motivation |
|----|---------------------|-----------|--------|-----------|
| SL 1 | Casual, opportunistic | Minimal | No IACS-specific knowledge | Curiosity, accidental |
| SL 2 | Deliberate, low sophistication | Limited | General IT skills, some IACS awareness | Financial gain, disruption |
| SL 3 | Sophisticated, targeted | Significant | IACS-specific expertise, inside knowledge | Espionage, sabotage, political |
| SL 4 | Nation-state, highly organized | Extensive | Deep IACS expertise, supply chain access | State objectives, critical infrastructure destruction |

### Three SL Types — Critical Distinction

| Type | Symbol | What It Represents | Who Determines It |
|------|--------|-------------------|------------------|
| Target SL | SL-T | Required security capability for a zone — the goal | Asset owner via SLRA (IEC 62443-3-2) |
| Capability SL | SL-C | Maximum security capability a product/system can achieve | Product supplier (IEC 62443-4-2) or integrator (IEC 62443-3-3) |
| Achieved SL | SL-A | Actual security level after implementation and configuration | Verified by asset owner or third party |

**The relationship that matters:** SL-A ≥ SL-T must always hold.

When SL-C of available components is less than SL-T, the gap must be closed through:
* Compensating controls at the zone or conduit level.
* Additional monitoring and detection.
* Operational controls (access restrictions, personnel procedures).
* Formal residual risk acceptance with management sign-off.

### Security Level Assignment Guidance

| Zone / System Type | Typical SL-T | Rationale |
|-------------------|-------------|-----------|
| Safety zone (SIS, ESD, F&G) | SL 3 | Compromise could cause physical harm or environmental disaster |
| Critical control (power grid primary, nuclear I&C) | SL 3 | National or regional impact of compromise |
| Standard process control (DCS, SCADA for production) | SL 2 | Deliberate attacks expected; targeted threat realistic |
| Supervisory systems (MES, historian) | SL 2 | Connected to multiple zones; entry point risk |
| Industrial DMZ | SL 2 | Boundary zone; must withstand targeted probing |
| Engineering zone | SL 2 | High-value target — write access to controllers |
| Lower Obligations environments (Decree 410) | SL 1–2 | Proportional to reduced regulatory scope and risk |

> Security Level assignment must be driven by risk assessment — not by this table alone. Use these as starting points for the SLRA, not as final answers.

## Foundational Requirements (FR) and System Requirements (SR)

IEC 62443-3-3 defines seven Foundational Requirements (FR), each decomposed into System Requirements (SR) with requirements specified per Security Level.

### FR 1 — Identification and Authentication Control (IAC)

**Principle:** All human users, software processes, and devices must be identified and authenticated before being granted access to IACS resources.

| SR | Requirement | SL 1 | SL 2 | SL 3 | SL 4 |
|----|-------------|------|------|------|------|
| SR 1.1 | Human user identification and authentication | ✓ Basic | ✓ + MFA for remote | ✓ + MFA all access | ✓ + strong MFA all |
| SR 1.2 | Software process and device identification | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| SR 1.3 | Account management | ✓ | ✓ + review process | ✓ + automated | ✓ + continuous |
| SR 1.4 | Identifier management | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| SR 1.5 | Authenticator management (passwords, certs) | ✓ Basic policy | ✓ + complexity | ✓ + PKI | ✓ + hardware tokens |
| SR 1.6 | Wireless access management | N/A | ✓ WPA2-Ent | ✓ WPA3-Ent + 802.1X | ✓ + continuous monitoring |
| SR 1.7 | Strength of password-based authentication | ✓ Basic | ✓ Enhanced | ✓ Strong | ✓ Maximum |
| SR 1.8 | PKI certificates | — | — | ✓ | ✓ |
| SR 1.9 | Strength of public key authentication | — | — | ✓ | ✓ |
| SR 1.10 | Authenticator feedback | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| SR 1.11 | Remote system identification | — | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| SR 1.12 | Unsuccessful authentication attempts | ✓ | ✓ + alerting | ✓ + lockout | ✓ + forensic |
| SR 1.13 | Access via untrusted networks | — | ✓ MFA + encryption | ✓ + PAM | ✓ + continuous verification |

**OT Implementation Notes for FR 1:**
* PLC/RTU controllers often do not support user-level authentication natively. Compensating controls: network access control at conduit level (allowlist by IP/MAC), physical access control to controller cabinets, engineering software authentication.
* MFA for OT workstations requires careful selection — hardware tokens or smart cards preferred over SMS/TOTP in high-security environments (offline-capable, no dependency on mobile network).
* Service accounts (OPC server connecting to PLC) require documented identity lifecycle management — these are frequently "forgotten" accounts with never-expiring credentials.

### FR 2 — Use Control (UC)

**Principle:** Authorized use of IACS resources must be enforced through access control; unauthorized use must be prevented.

| SR | Requirement | Key SL Escalation |
|----|-------------|------------------|
| SR 2.1 | Authorization enforcement (least privilege) | SL1: basic RBAC → SL3: dynamic, context-aware authorization |
| SR 2.2 | Wireless use control | SL2: controlled wireless → SL3: continuous monitoring |
| SR 2.3 | Use control for portable and mobile devices | SL1: policy → SL2: technical enforcement → SL3: DLP |
| SR 2.4 | Mobile code (scripts, macros) | SL2: whitelisting → SL3: signed code only |
| SR 2.5 | Session lock | SL1: timeout → SL2: + awareness → SL3: mandatory |
| SR 2.6 | Remote session termination | SL1: user-initiated → SL2: automatic timeout |
| SR 2.7 | Concurrent session control | SL2: limit concurrent sessions per user |
| SR 2.8 | Auditable events | SL1: log changes → SL2: log all privileged actions → SL3: comprehensive |
| SR 2.9 | Audit storage capacity | SL1: basic → SL2: protected capacity → SL3: WORM |
| SR 2.10 | Response to audit processing failures | SL2: alert → SL3: halt or degrade |
| SR 2.11 | Timestamps | SL1: system time → SL2: NTP synchronized → SL3: authenticated NTP |
| SR 2.12 | Non-repudiation | SL3: cryptographic non-repudiation of actions |

**OT Implementation Notes for FR 2:**
* RBAC in SCADA/DCS: define roles carefully — operator (read + setpoint adjustment), supervisor (operator + acknowledge alarms), engineer (supervisor + configuration changes), administrator (full access). Do not collapse roles.
* Portable device control is critical: engineering laptops connecting to OT bring IT malware into the control zone. Technical enforcement: only approved devices with OT security baseline; no personal devices; scan before connection.
* Audit log capacity: OT logging infrastructure is frequently underdimensioned. Calculate required storage based on event volume × retention period. Include firewall logs, SCADA audit logs, DCS event logs, engineering software audit trails.

---

### FR 3 — System Integrity (SI)

**Principle:** The integrity of the IACS must be protected against unauthorized modification of hardware, software, and data.

| SR | Requirement | Key SL Escalation |
|----|-------------|------------------|
| SR 3.1 | Communication integrity (transmission) | SL2: CRC/hash → SL3: cryptographic integrity (TLS, MACsec) |
| SR 3.2 | Malicious code protection | SL1: AV on hosts → SL2: + application whitelisting → SL3: + behavior monitoring |
| SR 3.3 | Security functionality verification | SL2: periodic check → SL3: continuous integrity monitoring |
| SR 3.4 | Software and information integrity | SL2: hash verification → SL3: code signing |
| SR 3.5 | Input validation | SL2: validate all external inputs → SL3: + anomaly detection |
| SR 3.6 | Deterministic output | SL3: verify outputs are deterministic under manipulation |
| SR 3.7 | Error handling | SL1: fail-safe → SL2: + logging → SL3: + tamper detection |
| SR 3.8 | Session integrity | SL2: session binding → SL3: + continuous validation |
| SR 3.9 | Protection of audit information | SL2: access control → SL3: WORM + integrity verification |

**OT Implementation Notes for FR 3:**
* Application whitelisting on OT hosts (SCADA server, engineering workstation, HMI PC): tools such as Tripwire, McAfee Application Control, or Windows AppLocker. Critical: test extensively before deployment — OT applications have unusual file access patterns that generate false positives.
* PLC program integrity: periodically compare runtime PLC program hash against known-good baseline. Tools: vendor-specific (Siemens TIA Portal audit, Rockwell FactoryTalk AssetCentre), or OT monitoring platforms (Dragos, Claroty, Nozomi).
* Transmission integrity in legacy OT: Modbus TCP has no built-in integrity protection. Compensating controls: monitor for unexpected function codes or register writes; isolate Modbus traffic within zones; implement at-rest integrity checks on PLC configuration.

---

### FR 4 — Data Confidentiality (DC)

**Principle:** Information in IACS that requires protection must be protected against unauthorized disclosure.

| SR | Requirement | Key SL Escalation |
|----|-------------|------------------|
| SR 4.1 | Information confidentiality (transmission) | SL2: encrypt sensitive paths → SL3: encrypt all control traffic |
| SR 4.2 | Information persistence | SL2: secure deletion → SL3: + cryptographic erasure |
| SR 4.3 | Use of cryptography | SL2: TLS 1.2+ → SL3: TLS 1.3 + certificate pinning |

**OT Implementation Notes for FR 4:**
* Confidentiality is lower priority than integrity and availability in most OT environments — but it matters for: remote access sessions (credentials, commands), engineering file transfers (PLC programs contain process IP), historian data (production rates, recipes, efficiency data).
* Legacy OT protocols (Modbus, DNP3 without authentication, OPC DA) transmit data in cleartext. Compensating control: VPN or TLS tunnel wrapping legacy protocol traffic where architecture allows; otherwise rely on network isolation.

---

### FR 5 — Restricted Data Flow (RDF)

**Principle:** IACS shall restrict the flow of information to only what is required for authorized operation.

| SR | Requirement | Key SL Escalation |
|----|-------------|------------------|
| SR 5.1 | Network segmentation | SL1: basic separation → SL2: zone/conduit model → SL3: application-layer filtering |
| SR 5.2 | Zone boundary protection | SL1: firewall → SL2: stateful + allowlist → SL3: deep packet inspection |
| SR 5.3 | General purpose person-to-person communication restrictions | SL2: no general internet from OT → SL3: no outbound initiation |
| SR 5.4 | Application partitioning | SL2: separate critical from non-critical functions → SL3: hardware isolation |

**OT Implementation Notes for FR 5:**
* This FR directly drives the zone/conduit architecture. Every communication between zones must pass through a defined conduit — this is the core architectural principle of IEC 62443.
* Deep packet inspection for OT protocols: dedicated OT firewalls (Claroty xDome Secure Access, Fortinet FortiGate Rugged, Cisco IE-based solutions, Moxa) or OT-capable NGFWs that understand Modbus, DNP3, EtherNet/IP, PROFINET at application layer.
* Application partitioning at SL 3/4: safety systems (SIS) must be on physically separate hardware from basic process control (BPCS) — not just logically separated on shared infrastructure.

---

### FR 6 — Timely Response to Events (TRE)

**Principle:** The IACS shall respond to security violations by notifying the appropriate authority, reporting needed information, and taking timely corrective action.

| SR | Requirement | Key SL Escalation |
|----|-------------|------------------|
| SR 6.1 | Audit log accessibility | SL1: log available → SL2: centralized, searchable → SL3: real-time SIEM |
| SR 6.2 | Continuous monitoring | SL2: automated monitoring → SL3: 24/7 with alerting → SL4: behavioral analytics |

**OT Implementation Notes for FR 6:**
* NTP synchronization is a prerequisite for meaningful audit logs. Without synchronized time, log correlation across SCADA, DCS, firewall, and engineering workstation is unreliable.
* OT-specific SIEM/NDR tools (Dragos Platform, Claroty, Nozomi Networks) understand OT protocol context — a Modbus Write Single Coil to a specific register means something in process context; a generic SIEM sees only a TCP packet.
* Detection content for OT: develop detection rules for OT-specific threat techniques from MITRE ATT&CK for ICS (see dedicated section).

---

### FR 7 — Resource Availability (RA)

**Principle:** The IACS shall ensure availability of resources in a timely fashion, including those needed to maintain operation of the IACS even in the presence of a denial-of-service attack.

| SR | Requirement | Key SL Escalation |
|----|-------------|------------------|
| SR 7.1 | Denial of service protection | SL1: rate limiting → SL2: DoS-resistant design → SL3: redundancy |
| SR 7.2 | Resource management | SL2: resource quotas → SL3: prioritization for control traffic |
| SR 7.3 | Control system backup | SL1: periodic backup → SL2: tested recovery → SL3: rapid recovery capability |
| SR 7.4 | Emergency power | SL2: UPS → SL3: generator + UPS |
| SR 7.5 | Emergency communication | SL2: backup communication path |
| SR 7.6 | Network and security configuration settings | SL2: documented and protected configs |
| SR 7.7 | Least functionality | SL2: disable unused services → SL3: verified minimal footprint |
| SR 7.8 | Control system component inventory | SL2: maintained inventory → SL3: automated |

**OT Implementation Notes for FR 7:**
* DoS protection in OT: industrial switches with storm control and rate limiting prevent broadcast storms from crashing PLC communication. This is a basic but frequently missing control.
* Least functionality is critical but dangerous without testing: disabling unused services (FTP, Telnet, HTTP management interfaces on PLCs) reduces attack surface significantly. But must be tested in staging environment — some "unused" services turn out to be required by undocumented integrations.
* Control system backup (SR 7.3) must include PLC programs, DCS configurations, SCADA projects, and engineering workstation images — not only server-level backups.

# Security Level Risk Assessment (IEC 62443-3-2) — Complete Process

The SLRA is the central analytical activity that links risk to architecture. It must be performed before technology selection.

## Process Overview

### Step 1: Define the System Under Consideration (SuC)

Establish the boundary of the analysis:
* What physical systems and locations are included?
* What processes does the SuC control?
* What are the operational and safety impacts of a compromise?
* What are the integration points with external systems?

Document the SuC definition. Scope creep during assessment is a common failure mode.

### Step 2: Perform Initial Zone and Conduit Partitioning

Based on the ISA-95/Purdue functional model and existing or planned system architecture, identify initial zone candidates. This is an iterative process — zones will be refined as the assessment proceeds.

Initial partitioning criteria:
* Physical location and access requirements.
* System function and trust requirements.
* Communication patterns (which systems need to talk to which?).
* Operational criticality and process safety impact.

### Step 3: Asset Identification and Criticality Assessment

For each zone, identify all assets:
* Hardware: controllers, workstations, network devices, safety systems, field devices.
* Software: SCADA applications, DCS runtime, historian, engineering software.
* Communication: protocols, network connections, integration points.

Assign criticality based on:
* Safety impact (can compromise cause harm to people?).
* Production impact (what is the financial consequence of unavailability?).
* Environmental impact.
* Recovery time (how long does it take to restore function?).

### Step 4: Threat Analysis

Identify relevant threats for each zone. Use structured threat analysis methods:

**Threat actor categories (aligned to SL definitions):**
| Category | Profile | Relevant SL |
|----------|---------|------------|
| Insider — accidental | Employee making mistakes | SL 1 |
| Insider — malicious | Disgruntled employee with access | SL 2 |
| External opportunistic | Script kiddie, ransomware as-a-service | SL 2 |
| Targeted external | Criminal organization, industrial espionage | SL 2–3 |
| Nation-state APT | State-sponsored, sector-specific targeting | SL 3–4 |

**Use MITRE ATT&CK for ICS** for structured threat enumeration (see dedicated section).

**OT-specific threat scenarios:**
* Ransomware propagating from IT to OT through misconfigured IT/OT boundary.
* Malware introduced via engineering laptop or USB media.
* Compromised remote access account (vendor VPN credential theft).
* Supply chain compromise (malicious firmware update, trojanized software).
* Insider modification of PLC program.
* Denial of service attack disrupting PLC communication (broadcast storm).
* Exploitation of unpatched vulnerability in SCADA server.
* Man-in-the-middle on Modbus/EtherNet/IP communication (unauthorized command injection).

### Step 5: Vulnerability Identification

For each asset and threat, identify relevant vulnerabilities:
* Known CVEs in installed software/firmware versions.
* Configuration weaknesses (default passwords, unnecessary services enabled).
* Architectural weaknesses (missing segmentation, flat OT network).
* Process weaknesses (lack of change management, missing access reviews).
* Physical weaknesses (uncontrolled physical access to PLC cabinets).

Sources: ICS-CERT advisories, vendor security bulletins, NVD, NÚKIB varování.

### Step 6: Risk Assessment

For each threat scenario × zone combination, assess:
* **Likelihood:** given existing controls, how probable is successful exploitation?
* **Consequence:** safety impact + production impact + environmental impact + regulatory impact.
* **Risk level:** combination of likelihood and consequence (risk matrix or quantitative method).

### Step 7: Determine Security Level Target (SL-T) per Zone

Based on risk assessment results, assign SL-T to each zone. The SL-T reflects the level of protection required to reduce risk to an acceptable level.

Decision logic:
* If risk is acceptable with SL 1 controls → SL-T = 1.
* If risk requires resistance against deliberate low-sophistication attacks → SL-T = 2.
* If critical safety or national infrastructure is involved with sophisticated threat actors → SL-T = 3.
* SL 4 is reserved for extreme cases (typically nuclear, defense-adjacent).

### Step 8: Identify Security Measures

For each zone with an assigned SL-T, identify the security measures required to achieve that SL-T:
* Technical controls (firewall configuration, authentication, encryption).
* Operational controls (access procedures, change management, monitoring).
* Physical controls (cabinet locks, access badges, camera surveillance).
* Compensating controls for legacy systems that cannot achieve SL-T natively.

### Step 9: Verify Residual Risk

After applying security measures:
* What is the Achieved SL (SL-A) for each zone?
* Is SL-A ≥ SL-T?
* Is residual risk acceptable to management?

Document residual risk and obtain formal management acceptance (signed risk acceptance record).

### Step 10: Document, Review, and Maintain

The SLRA is a living document:
* Update when new assets are added to zones.
* Update when new vulnerabilities are discovered.
* Update when system changes affect zone boundaries or conduits.
* Review minimum annually for Higher Obligations environments.
* Trigger immediate review after a security incident.

# Defense in Depth — Layered Security Architecture

IEC 62443 promotes defense in depth: no single control is relied upon exclusively, and a weakness in any layer must be compensated by strength in adjacent, independent layers. # Secure Lifecycle — Detailed Phase Guide

Cybersecurity must be integrated at every lifecycle phase. The cost of addressing security issues increases by an order of magnitude with each lifecycle stage at which they are discovered.

## Phase 1: Concept

**Activities:**
* Define security objectives and constraints for the project.
* Identify regulatory obligations (Czech Cybersecurity Act, sector-specific regulation).
* Determine applicable obligation regime (Higher or Lower).
* Initial asset scoping — what systems will the project include?
* Identify functional safety requirements (IEC 61511) and their interaction with cybersecurity.

**Outputs:**
* Security objectives statement.
* Regulatory compliance requirements.
* Initial risk appetite definition.

**Common mistake:** Starting with a technology selection ("we'll use Siemens TIA Portal and AVEVA System Platform") before understanding security requirements. Technology must follow architecture, and architecture must follow risk.

---

## Phase 2: Design

**Activities:**
* Perform Security Level Risk Assessment (IEC 62443-3-2).
* Define Security Zones and Conduits.
* Assign Security Level Targets (SL-T) per zone.
* Design architecture to achieve SL-T.
* Define security requirements for procured components (feeding into IEC 62443-4-2 evaluation).
* Define security requirements for system integrator (feeding into IEC 62443-2-4 evaluation).
* Coordinate with functional safety design (IEC 61511) — joint HAZOP/cyber review for safety-critical zones.

**Outputs:**
* Security architecture document with zone/conduit diagram.
* SLRA report with SL-T assignments.
* Security requirements specification (for procurement).
* As-designed architecture documentation (baseline).

**Architecture decision records:** Document why each architectural decision was made. Future engineers will ask "why is there a data diode here instead of a firewall?" — the decision record answers that question and prevents regression.

---

## Phase 3: Procurement and Supplier Qualification

**Activities:**
* Evaluate system integrators against IEC 62443-2-4.
* Evaluate product suppliers (PLC vendors, SCADA vendors) against IEC 62443-4-1/4-2.
* Include security requirements in RFP and contracts.
* Establish supplier security requirements (incident notification, access management, SBOM).

**Supplier evaluation criteria:**
* IEC 62443-4-1 certification (by accredited third party) for product suppliers.
* IEC 62443-2-4 compliance evidence for system integrators.
* Documented vulnerability disclosure process (PSIRT).
* Track record of security advisories and timely patches.
* SBOM availability for delivered software/firmware.

**Contract security provisions:**
* Security incident notification obligations.
* Access governance requirements for project personnel.
* Subcontracting security requirements (pass-through obligations).
* Right to audit security practices.
* Liability for security defects introduced during integration.

---

## Phase 4: Implementation

**Activities:**
* System hardening according to vendor hardening guides and IEC 62443-4-2 requirements.
* Security control configuration (firewall rules, IAM configuration, logging setup).
* Disable unnecessary services, default accounts, and unused network interfaces.
* Apply security baselines before network connection.
* Implement and configure OT monitoring tools.
* Staff training and awareness.

**Hardening reference — common OT components:**

| Component | Key Hardening Steps |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Windows-based SCADA server | Remove unnecessary roles and features; apply CIS benchmark; disable Guest; rename Administrator; enable Windows Firewall; deploy application whitelisting; configure audit logging |
| Siemens S7 PLC | Enable access protection (password on OB1 / CPU access levels); disable unused communication ports (e.g., PUT/GET if not needed); enable PLC program protection; configure message logging |
| Rockwell ControlLogix | Configure controller security (FactoryTalk Security); enable motion safety passwords; configure CIP Safety; restrict Ethernet/IP connections by source IP |
| Historian server | Separate read and write interfaces; use read-only replica in DMZ; restrict SQL access; configure TLS for OPC connections |
| Industrial switch (Hirschmann, Moxa) | Change default credentials; disable SNMP v1/v2c (use SNMPv3 with auth+priv); enable port security; disable unused ports; configure 802.1X if available; enable HTTPS management only |
| Industrial firewall | Allowlist-only ruleset; log all traffic; enable application-layer OT protocol inspection if available; configure IPS signatures for ICS protocols |

---

## Phase 5: Commissioning

**Activities:**
* Verify Achieved Security Level (SL-A) against Security Level Target (SL-T) for each zone.
* Conduct security acceptance testing:
  * Firewall rule verification (confirm only authorized traffic passes).
  * Authentication testing (confirm MFA works, weak credentials rejected).
  * Monitoring verification (confirm logs arrive, alerts fire correctly).
  * Backup and recovery test (confirm procedures work in practice).
* Penetration testing or security assessment (scope carefully — active testing in OT requires expert judgment to avoid production impact).
* Document as-built architecture (any differences from as-designed must be recorded and justified).
* Hand over security documentation package to asset owner operations team.

**SL-A verification method:**
For each FR/SR applicable to the zone's SL-T, verify that the control is implemented and effective. This can be done through:
* Documentation review (policies, configurations).
* Configuration inspection (firewall rules, access control lists).
* Functional testing (attempt unauthorized access, trigger monitoring alerts).
* Third-party assessment (independent verification for Higher Obligations environments).

---

## Phase 6: Operation and Maintenance

**Activities:**
* Continuous monitoring (OT IDS, log analysis, alerting).
* Patch management (IEC 62443-2-3 process).
* Change management — every change to OT systems must go through formal CM process.
* Periodic access reviews (user accounts, vendor access, service accounts).
* Periodic risk review (minimum annually, trigger-based otherwise).
* Security awareness and training (annual refresher, role-specific for engineers/operators/managers).
* Supplier access management (track active access sessions, revoke when work is complete).

**OT Change Management — critical differences from IT:**
* Every change to PLC program or DCS configuration must be reviewed and approved.
* Test in staging/simulation environment before production deployment.
* Maintain version control for PLC programs and DCS configurations.
* Change window coordination with production (shutdown required? partial operation?).
* Rollback plan must exist before any change is made.
* Post-change verification — confirm system behaves as expected.

---

## Phase 7: Modification and Upgrade

**Activities:**
* Re-assess risk when modifications change zone boundaries, assets, or trust relationships.
* Apply IEC 62443-3-2 SLRA to modified system under consideration.
* Evaluate new components against IEC 62443-4-2 before introduction.
* Update security documentation (zone diagram, asset inventory, risk assessment).
* Conduct security testing after modification.

> A system modification without a security re-assessment is a common source of compliance gaps and architectural regression. Adding a new integration point, even a seemingly minor one, can violate zone/conduit assumptions.

---

## Phase 8: Decommissioning

**Activities:**
* Secure data destruction — PLC programs, DCS configurations, historian data may contain sensitive process information.
* Revoke all access (user accounts, vendor credentials, remote access).
* Remove from asset inventory.
* Update documentation and risk assessment.
* Recover physical media for secure disposal.

# Relationship with Functional Safety (IEC 61511)

The intersection of cybersecurity (IEC 62443) and functional safety (IEC 61511 / IEC 61508) is one of the highest-stakes areas in OT security architecture.

## Key Principles

* **Safety functions must be maintained under cyber attack.** A successful attack on a SIS that prevents safety response is potentially catastrophic.
* **Security measures must not impair safety functions.** An access control measure that delays or prevents operator response to a safety demand is itself a safety hazard.
* **Independent assessment is required.** The Security Level assessment of SIS and safety-related zones must be conducted independently from the basic process control system.
* **Coordinate the two disciplines from the start.** Security engineers and safety engineers must work together — their requirements can conflict and must be resolved at design stage.

## Practical Integration Points

| Activity | Functional Safety | Cybersecurity | Integration Point |
|----------|------------------|---------------|------------------|
| HAZOP | Identifies process hazards and initiating events | Adds cyber-initiated events as initiating events | Joint HAZOP with cyber scenarios |
| LOPA | Validates independence of protection layers | SIS must maintain independence even under cyber attack | Independence analysis must consider cyber defeat |
| SIL verification | Verifies SIS achieves required SIL | SIS must achieve SL-T even under threat | Combined security/safety verification |
| Management of change | Safety MOC for SIS changes | Security MOC for any BPCS/SIS changes | Unified change management process |
| Proof test | Periodic testing of SIS safety functions | Security testing must not interfere with SIS | Coordinated test scheduling |

## Reference Incident — Triton/TRISIS (2017)

Attackers (attributed to TEMP.Veles, a Russian nation-state group) compromised a Triconex Safety Instrumented System at a petrochemical facility in Saudi Arabia. The attack used a custom-developed malware framework (TRITON) that:
* Gained access to the Triconex engineering workstation.
* Deployed malicious ladder logic programs to Triconex Trident controllers.
* Attempted to reprogram the SIS to either fail-safe (causing plant shutdown) or fail-to-danger (disabling safety protections).

The attack was only discovered because a coding error in the TRITON malware caused the SIS to enter a safe state and initiate a plant shutdown — drawing attention to the compromise.

**Architectural lessons from Triton:**
* Engineering workstations for SIS must be in a separate, highly restricted zone.
* SIS controllers must have physical separation from BPCS — not only logical.
* Firmware integrity of SIS controllers should be periodically verified.
* SIS communication must be monitored — even read-only monitoring can detect anomalous communication patterns.
* Access to SIS engineering software must require elevated authorization separate from BPCS access.

# IEC 62443 and the Purdue/ISA-95 Reference Models

The three reference models address different architectural concerns and must be used together.

| Model | Addresses | Primary Question Answered |
|-------|-----------|--------------------------|
| ISA-95 | Functional architecture and business integration | Which systems exist? What information flows where? Who is responsible for which function? |
| Purdue Model (PERA) | Network trust levels and connectivity | Where do trust boundaries exist? What connects to what? |
| IEC 62443 | Security requirements for systems and zones | What security capabilities are required? How do we verify them? |

**Working sequence:**
1. Use ISA-95 to understand functional architecture and information ownership (see [ISA95.md](ISA95.md)).
2. Use the Purdue Model to identify initial zone and conduit candidates based on network trust levels (see [Purdue-Model.md](Purdue-Model.md)).
3. Use IEC 62443-3-2 (SLRA) to refine zones, assign SL-T, and define conduit requirements.
4. Use IEC 62443-3-3 to define detailed security requirements per zone.
5. Use IEC 62443-4-2 to evaluate whether selected components meet SL-T.

> **Historical note.** The first edition of IEC 62443-1-1 included a hierarchical diagram derived from the Purdue/ISA-95 work, and the series adopted that level hierarchy as the structural basis for its zones. Later revisions have deliberately **de-emphasized the direct link to PERA in favour of the zone-and-conduit model**, because rigid level hierarchies map poorly onto modern IIoT, edge and cloud-connected architectures. Zones and conduits — not Purdue levels — are the authoritative IEC 62443 construct (see *Zones and Conduits* above); the Purdue Model remains a useful *input* for identifying candidate zones, not a mandated topology.

# Certification and Conformance

## IEC 62443 Certification Schemes

| Scope | Standard | Certification Body Examples |
|-------|----------|----------------------------|
| Product (component) security | IEC 62443-4-1 + 4-2 | TÜV Rheinland, TÜV SÜD, exida, UL |
| System integration process | IEC 62443-2-4 | TÜV Rheinland, ISASecure |
| Asset owner security program | IEC 62443-2-1 | exida, TÜV SÜD |

## ISASecure

ISASecure is an independent certification scheme for IEC 62443 administered by ISCI (ISA Security Compliance Institute). Provides:
* EDSA (Embedded Device Security Assurance) — for component suppliers (IEC 62443-4-2).
* SSA (System Security Assurance) — for system integrators and asset owners.
* SDLA (Security Development Lifecycle Assurance) — for development organizations (IEC 62443-4-1).

**Asset owner use:** Prefer ISASecure-certified products when SL-T ≥ 2 and the supplier has obtained certification. This provides independent third-party verification rather than relying solely on supplier self-declaration.

## Certification vs. Compliance

Certification is not the same as compliance:
* A certified product meets defined security capability requirements (SL-C).
* Compliance requires that the deployed system achieves the required Security Level (SL-A ≥ SL-T).
* A certified component in a poorly architected system does not produce a compliant system.

# Common Misconceptions

IEC 62443 is **not**:

* A certification that makes a system completely secure.
* A firewall configuration guide or PLC hardening checklist.
* A substitute for risk assessment — it provides the methodology, not the answers.
* A replacement for operational governance and process discipline.
* An enterprise IT security framework applied to OT — the OT context requires fundamentally different trade-offs.
* A one-time activity — it requires continuous application throughout the system lifecycle.
* Equivalent to ISO 27001 for OT — IEC 62443 addresses operational technology specifically; ISO 27001 addresses information management.

It is an engineering methodology for systematic, risk-based, lifecycle-aware IACS security.

# Typical IEC 62443-Compliant OT Architecture Summary

A mature OT architecture informed by IEC 62443 will include:

* **Zone and conduit design** with documented SL-T per zone and enforced conduit boundaries.
* **Industrial DMZ** as the mandatory and only path between IT and OT networks.
* **Safety zone** physically separated from BPCS with hardware data diode for monitoring-only communication.
* **Engineering zone** separate from control zone; engineering workstations under PAM.
* **Unidirectional gateways** for OT→IT data flows without required return channel.
* **Allowlist firewall rules** at all zone boundaries; deny-by-default; every rule documented.
* **Centralized IAM** with unique accounts, RBAC, MFA for remote/privileged access, vendor access management.
* **Passive OT monitoring / IDS** with OT protocol understanding; NTP-synchronized centralized logging.
* **OT-adapted vulnerability management** with staging environment for patch testing.
* **Air-gapped configuration backups** with tested recovery procedures and defined RTO/RPO.
* **OT-specific Incident Response Plan** coordinated with safety response and NÚKIB notification.
* **Supplier governance** — IEC 62443-2-4 for integrators, IEC 62443-4-1/4-2 for product suppliers.
* **Living documentation** — as-built architecture, SLRA, asset inventory, all maintained continuously.

# Architect Sequence

Experienced OT architects follow this sequence. Deviating from it — especially by starting with technology selection — predictably produces insecure architectures.

```
Step 1: Understand the industrial process and safety requirements
         │  Output: Process description, safety function inventory
Step 2: Identify critical assets and their functions (ISA-95 lens)
         │  Output: Initial asset inventory, functional architecture
Step 3: Perform SLRA (IEC 62443-3-2)
         │  Output: Threat analysis, risk assessment, SL-T per zone
Step 4: Define Zones and Conduits
         │  Output: Zone/conduit diagram, zone security policy
Step 5: Design architecture to meet SL-T
         │  Output: Security architecture document
Step 6: Select technologies
         │  Output: Technology decisions (informed by architecture)
Step 7: Qualify suppliers (IEC 62443-2-4 / 4-1 / 4-2)
         │  Output: Supplier qualification records
Step 8: Implement and harden
         │  Output: As-built configuration documentation
Step 9: Verify Achieved SL (SL-A ≥ SL-T)
         │  Output: Security verification report
Step 10: Document, monitor, and maintain
         │  Output: Operational security documentation set
```

> Technology selection is Step 6 — not Step 1. Any project that starts with "we'll use [product X]" and then tries to fit security around it is doing this in the wrong order.

**Analýza zranitelností**
- [ ] Byly prohledány CVE databáze a vendor advisories pro všechny relevantní systémy
- [ ] Byly identifikovány konfigurační slabiny (výchozí hesla, zbytečné služby)
- [ ] Byly identifikovány architektonické slabiny (chybějící segmentace, flat sítě, přímé IT↔OT propojení)

**Hodnocení rizik a SL-T**
- [ ] Bylo provedeno hodnocení rizik (pravděpodobnost × dopad) pro každý scénář a zónu
- [ ] Byl každé zóně přiřazen Security Level Target (SL-T) na základě hodnocení rizik
- [ ] Safety zóna (SIS) má SL-T přiřazen samostatně a nezávisle
- [ ] Byl SL-T odsouhlasen managementem

**Opatření a zbytkové riziko**
- [ ] Byla identifikována bezpečnostní opatření pro dosažení SL-T v každé zóně
- [ ] Byl odhadnut Achieved SL (SL-A) po implementaci opatření
- [ ] Je SL-A ≥ SL-T pro každou zónu?
- [ ] Bylo zbytkové riziko zdokumentováno a formálně akceptováno managementem (podpis)

**Dokumentace**
- [ ] Je SLRA zdokumentován ve formě, která umožňuje audit (vstupní data, metodika, výstupy, rozhodnutí)
- [ ] Existuje plán aktualizace SLRA (roční přezkum + trigger-based při změnách)

## Checklist 3: Návrh zón a kanálů (Zone and Conduit Design)

Použij při návrhu bezpečnostní architektury.

### Česky

**Definice zón**
- [ ] Byla Safety zóna (SIS, ESD, F&G) definována jako samostatná, fyzicky separovaná zóna
- [ ] Byla Engineering zóna definována odděleně od řídicí zóny
- [ ] Byla Industrial DMZ definována jako povinná hranice IT/OT
- [ ] Každá zóna má přiřazen SL-T (z výstupu SLRA)
- [ ] Každá zóna má zdokumentovanou bezpečnostní politiku (kdo a co smí)
- [ ] Aktiva se stejnými bezpečnostními požadavky jsou seskupena ve stejné zóně

**Definice kanálů (Conduits)**
- [ ] Každá mezizonální komunikace prochází definovaným kanálem — žádné neřízené cesty
- [ ] Každý kanál má zdokumentovaný provozní důvod, vlastníka a seznam povolených komunikací
- [ ] Jsou firewallová pravidla na allowlist bázi (deny-by-default) na všech hranicích zón
- [ ] Jsou jednosměrné brány (data diodes) použity tam, kde zpětný kanál není provozně nutný
- [ ] Je OPC DA (DCOM) nahrazen OPC UA s bezpečnostním profilem (Sign and Encrypt) nebo je komunikace terminována na OPC aggregation serveru v DMZ

**Ověření absence neřízených cest**
- [ ] Neexistuje žádné přímé propojení IT ↔ OT mimo průmyslovou DMZ
- [ ] Neexistuje žádný modem, 3G/4G router nebo jiný alternativní přístupový bod bez evidence a řízení
- [ ] Neexistuje žádné bezdrátové zařízení v OT síti bez definované bezdrátové zóny a WPA3-Enterprise autentizace
- [ ] Historián je v MOM zóně nebo vyšší — nikoli přímo v řídicí zóně s PLC
- [ ] Jump server pro vzdálený přístup je v Industrial DMZ, nikoli přímo v řídicí zóně

**Dokumentace**
- [ ] Byl vytvořen zone/conduit diagram zahrnující všechny zóny, kanály a SL-T
- [ ] Jsou zdokumentovány všechna povolená mezizonální spojení (zdrojová IP, cílová IP, port, protokol, směr)
- [ ] Diagram odpovídá skutečné konfiguraci (as-built, ne as-designed)

## Checklist 4: Výběr a kvalifikace dodavatelů

Použij při hodnocení systémových integrátorů a výrobců OT produktů.

### Česky

**Systémový integrátor (IEC 62443-2-4)**
- [ ] Má integrátor dokumentované procesy pro bezpečné provádění OT projektů?
- [ ] Prokázal integrátor shodu s IEC 62443-2-4 (certifikace nebo zdokumentované procesy)?
- [ ] Má integrátor zkušenosti s OT projekty v relevantním sektoru?
- [ ] Jsou v projektové smlouvě zahrnuty bezpečnostní povinnosti integrátora?
- [ ] Je definován postup pro hlášení bezpečnostních incidentů integrátora zákazníkovi?
- [ ] Je zakázán subdodavatelský přístup bez souhlasu zákazníka?

**Výrobci OT produktů (IEC 62443-4-1 / 4-2)**
- [ ] Má výrobce certifikaci IEC 62443-4-1 (secure development lifecycle)?
- [ ] Má konkrétní produkt certifikaci IEC 62443-4-2 na odpovídající SL-C ≥ SL-T zóny?
- [ ] Má výrobce zdokumentovaný proces pro zveřejňování a řešení zranitelností (PSIRT)?
- [ ] Má výrobce historii vydávání bezpečnostních záplat v přiměřených lhůtách?
- [ ] Je k dispozici SBOM (Software Bill of Materials) pro dodávaný produkt?
- [ ] Je definována délka bezpečnostní podpory produktu (EoL / EoSS datum)?

## Checklist 5: Komisioning a ověření bezpečnosti (SL-A Verification)

Použij před předáním OT systému do provozu.

### Česky

**Ověření síťové architektury**
- [ ] Byla ověřena firewall pravidla — odpovídají zdokumentovaným povoleným komunikacím?
- [ ] Byl proveden test průchodu neoprávněného provozu (negativní test — pokus o připojení přes nepovolený port)
- [ ] Bylo ověřeno, že Industrial DMZ funguje jako zamýšlená hranice (žádný přímý IT↔OT průchod)
- [ ] Byla ověřena funkčnost data diod (jednosměrnost — pokus o odesílání dat zpětným směrem)

**Ověření IAM**
- [ ] Byla odstraněna všechna výchozí hesla (default credentials) na všech zařízeních
- [ ] Byly odstraněny nebo deaktivovány výchozí systémové účty (administrator, guest, root)
- [ ] Bylo ověřeno, že MFA funguje pro vzdálený přístup a privilegované operace
- [ ] Byl ověřen RBAC — uživatel v roli "operator" nemá oprávnění provádět engineering úkony

**Ověření monitoringu a logování**
- [ ] Jsou logy ze všech klíčových OT systémů doručovány do centrálního log systému?
- [ ] Je NTP synchronizace funkční na všech OT zařízeních (ověřit stratum a drift)?
- [ ] Jsou alertovací pravidla aktivní a testována (test alert na neautorizovaný přístup)?
- [ ] Je integrita logů chráněna (WORM nebo kryptografické podepisování)?

**Ověření záloh a obnovy**
- [ ] Existují zálohy PLC programů, DCS konfigurací, SCADA projektů?
- [ ] Byl proveden test obnovy — byl systém úspěšně obnoven ze zálohy v testovacím prostředí?
- [ ] Jsou offline zálohy kritických konfigurací fyzicky uloženy mimo produkční síť?

**Dokumentace komisioning**
- [ ] Byl vytvořen as-built architektonický diagram odpovídající skutečné implementaci
- [ ] Jsou zdokumentovány všechny odchylky od as-designed architektury s odůvodněním
- [ ] Byl aktualizován inventář aktiv o všechny nainstalované komponenty
- [ ] Byla provedena finální SLRA aktualizace pro as-built stav — je SL-A ≥ SL-T pro každou zónu?

## Checklist 6: Průběžná bezpečnostní provozní připravenost

Použij jako pravidelný (čtvrtletní nebo roční) review provozního stavu bezpečnosti.

### Česky

**Správa aktiv**
- [ ] Je inventář OT aktiv aktuální (přidaná zařízení, vyřazená zařízení, změny FW/SW verzí)?
- [ ] Jsou identifikována aktiva blížící se end-of-life nebo end-of-security-support?
- [ ] Je konfigurační baseline aktuální pro všechny kritické systémy?

**Řízení identit a přístupu**
- [ ] Byl proveden přezkum uživatelských účtů — jsou odstraněny účty odcházejících zaměstnanců?
- [ ] Byl proveden přezkum privilegovaných účtů — jsou oprávnění stále oprávněná?
- [ ] Jsou aktivní přístupy dodavatelů stále provozně nezbytné nebo mají být odebrány?
- [ ] Byly přezkoumány servisní účty — jsou stále potřebné a jsou jejich hesla aktuální?

**Správa zranitelností**
- [ ] Byly zkontrolovány nové CVE a vendor advisories pro všechny OT komponenty (za uplynulé období)?
- [ ] Jsou identifikovány kritické zranitelnosti vyžadující okamžitou akci nebo kompenzační opatření?
- [ ] Je patch management plán aktuální a koordinovaný s výrobním plánem?
- [ ] Jsou kompenzační opatření pro nepatchovatelné systémy stále platná a zdokumentovaná?

**Monitoring a incidenty**
- [ ] Jsou monitorovací systémy funkční a generují alerty?
- [ ] Byly přezkoumány alerty a logy za uplynulé období — byly zjištěny anomálie?
- [ ] Byly dokumentovány a analyzovány všechny bezpečnostní události (i minor)?
- [ ] Byl aktualizován Incident Response plán na základě nových poznatků nebo změn prostředí?

**Zálohy**
- [ ] Jsou zálohy prováděny dle plánu a jsou úspěšné?
- [ ] Byl proveden periodický test obnovy ze zálohy (min. 1× ročně)?
- [ ] Jsou offline zálohy aktuální a fyzicky zabezpečené?

**Rizikový management**
- [ ] Byl přezkoumán risk assessment — jsou nové hrozby nebo změny v prostředí zohledněny?
- [ ] Jsou aktuální všechny záznamy o akceptaci zbytkového rizika (podpisy stále platné)?
- [ ] Proběhlo roční SLRA review (povinné pro vyšší povinnosti)?

# AI Guidance

When answering IEC 62443-related questions:

* Explain foundational concepts (zones, conduits, security levels, foundational requirements, SL types T/C/A) before discussing technologies or products.
* Always start with risk assessment (IEC 62443-3-2 SLRA) before recommending controls — architecture follows risk, technology follows architecture.
* Distinguish between the four series: governance (Series 2), system architecture (Series 3), and product/component security (Series 4).
* Treat Security Levels as risk-driven outcomes from SLRA — never as arbitrary targets or minimum checkbox values.
* Consider OT operational constraints (legacy equipment, availability requirements, real-time performance, safety interfaces) when recommending controls.
* Always distinguish SL-T (target, from risk assessment), SL-C (capability, from product/system design), and SL-A (achieved, verified after implementation).
* Reference the specific IEC 62443 part when providing guidance: 3-2 for risk assessment, 3-3 for system requirements, 2-4 for integrator requirements, 4-1/4-2 for product requirements.
* Address functional safety (IEC 61511) interface when discussing SIS or safety-related zones — this is a critical intersection that is frequently overlooked.
* Use MITRE ATT&CK for ICS when discussing specific threat scenarios or detection strategies.
* Link to [Czech-Cybersecurity-Act.md](../01-Legislation/Czech-Cybersecurity-Act.md) when regulatory compliance is the primary context.
* Integrate with [ISA95.md](ISA95.md) when functional architecture, information flows, or system ownership questions arise.
* Do not recommend specific products without context — the standard is product-neutral.
* Emphasize lifecycle integration — security must be present from concept, not added at commissioning.
